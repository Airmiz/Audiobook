*7 1691
The path into the city was little more than a dirt track that led to
three or four, simple, wooden buildings. The products of the trees that had
once been the path, they were rough cut, too simple, and less than uniform.
The village itself was no more than space forcibly taken from the forest, a
small clearing where the sky was visible for a hundred or so paces before it
was swallowed by pine and oak.
Standing in the middle of the path, staring at the tiny village, I didn’t
know what to do. I had a few minutes, maybe less, before a band of a
hundred or so Indians, armed to the teeth with strange looking swords, little
hatchets, and bows, burst from the forest like a swarm of angry hornets and
attacked. One of the buildings was a church with a little, two-story bell
tower. I took a breath and concentrated, I was weak, but I had to help. The
bell tower.
With a blink, I found myself inside the church, a space steeped in
history and hushed reverence. The place was little more than one large,
square room. There were two sets of four, straight-backed wooden pews, a
small wooden cross on the wall over a simple podium, and a prayer bench.
My knees were weak. I was exhausted. My failed attack on Toniat
had wiped me out. I tried to focus what strength I had left. Then I heard her
thoughts.
Is there even a god? I mean, do I really believe there is some
invisible, magical man somewhere keeping an eye on everything and
everyone? I think my mother was right, if there is a god, she’s a woman.
My mother was right. This separatist, puritan talk is nonsense. The old
ways. They hate the old ways.
I turned around to find a young woman, her eyes filled with a mix of
determination and sorrow, striding toward the front of the church. Her black
hair, streaked with hints of gray, framed a face that had seen hardship, yet
her posture spoke of resilience and unyielding faith.

She was tall and thin, in her early twenties with pitch, black hair,
fair skin, and green eyes. She was a beauty by anyone’s standards and, had
it been my own time, I imagine she would have been an actress or a model,
a woman with a string of men behind her, each trying to outspend the other
for her affections. But in the light of a church hundreds of years before the
advent of even the most basic luxuries, she was a beautiful woman in a hard
place. It showed in the expression on her face - the furrowed brows and
pursed lips. She was the picture of unhappiness, an angry woman in rigid
clothing and uncomfortable shoes.
Her footsteps reverberated through the room, each resonant tap
echoing my own uncertainty. Her eyes were furrowed in frustration. She
flattened the front of her plain, black dress with both hands as she walked
past me and I felt like I was in the middle of an historical reenactment from
a time period I knew almost nothing about. Where am I? I wondered.
I can’t believe I have to hide inside a church. How I wish I was a
man. If I were a man, I would have beaten him senseless last night. What
folly! What a foolish thing to do. How could he take a loan against a house
that isn’t even built yet? She reached the little prayer bench and stopped.
From my vantage at the back of the church, I observed her every
step, my empathy for her palpable. She was troubled and, instinctively,
something inside of me wanted to help. I wanted to ease her frustration,
wanted to put her mind at ease.
As she knelt and bowed her head, her whispered plea for protection
and prosperity hung in the air, making me ponder the beginning of such a
journey.
I wondered myself if he was up there. There were other gods, Toniat
and Muma had taught me that, but what about here and now? Was the god
that everyone always talked about, the god of the Bible and Christianity
real? What of Buddha? The devil? I started to follow the chain of thought
but then remembered why I had come to the church - the attack was
coming.
The bell tower seemed a later addition, a testament to the passage of
time since the church's first stones were laid. Behind the podium was a
small, plank door that led to the little, two-story tower that held the bell. I
slipped through the door.

The room was small and lit only by the light that made it past the
roof and the bell a few meters over my head. I thought of the approaching
Indians and the coming attack. I didn’t have the energy to stop them and,
even if I did, I wasn’t sure how. I had never done anything so grand before.
No, the only thing I could do was warn the village. I wrapped my hands
around the rope and pulled.
Within a moment, the bell was clanging back and forth alerting all
who could hear and vibrating every board and slat.
After half a minute, the little door to my right opened and the
woman who had been about to pray was staring at me. I knew I was
invisible, or was fairly sure, I mean she hadn’t seen me when she walked
into the church, but now.
A perplexed furrow creased her brow as her gaze swept from my
head to my feet, leaving me to wonder how much she truly saw. Who? Are
her eyes glowing? That robe, it’s so…and the symbols…are they moving?
What is she?
My breath caught as I spoke, urgency pushing each syllable out,
"Native Americans!" The words hung in the air, a plea for understanding
and action, a plea that I hoped would transcend the gulf between our
worlds.
Amid the anxiety-laden silence, her gaze met mine, a perplexed
crease furrowing her brow as if grappling with the urgency in my voice.
I questioned how much she could truly see - my otherworldly robe,
the symbols that seemed to dance upon it. Were my fears and purpose as
transparent as my presence?
Her gaze held mine for a fleeting moment before sliding away,
leaving a trail of uncertainty in its wake. She uttered no words, and though
her lips remained closed, I could almost hear the thoughts tumbling through
her mind, like a waterfall of curiosity and disbelief.
Then, a sudden intrusion. A man, aged by the passage of time yet
harboring a kindness in his demeanor, stepped into the doorway, his gaze
shifting between the woman and the bell in the tower above.
The woman's startled response was palpable, her discomfort evident
in the downward cast of her gaze. The interaction felt like an intimate dance

of shared secrets and unspoken emotions. She stared into my eyes and I
could tell that the words had rolled off of her like water.
“The town is about to be attacked! A group of Indians…”
Her gaze went from my face to my robe again and I was just about
to shift into something that her mind wouldn’t wrestle with when a man
stepped into the doorway behind her.
He was older, in his early fifties. His hair was cut short and gray and
his face, though hardened and wrinkled, held a certain kindness. He looked
at her and then up at the bell in the tower. “Hester?”
The woman almost jumped out of her skin. “Pastor.” She looked
down at the ground between them like she was embarrassed.
“What’s the meaning of this? Why have you rung the bell, child?”
Hester glanced over at where I stood and then back at the pastor.
“Indians,” she said. “Indians are about to attack the village.”
A flicker of concern washed over the pastor's features, his
complexion paling as if the gravity of the situation had solidified before
him. "Are you certain?" he queried, his voice catching on the edge of
desperation.
The woman's response held a steadfast resolve, her affirmation
unshaken, like a beacon amidst the encroaching shadows. "Yes," she
replied, the word carrying the weight of conviction.
The pastor's hands clenched the rope with renewed vigor, the bell's
resonating chime punctuating the air with each forceful pull. But then, as if
struck by a lightning of thought, his gaze shifted to Hester. "Remain here,
Hester," he instructed, his words a mixture of command and concern.
"Guard the tower, but do not sound the bell." A purposeful nod, a hurried
closing of the door, and his retreating footsteps marked his departure.
As the pastor vanished from view, the sudden hush of silence
intensified the building tension. My presence, once a secret observer, now
felt like a shadow in plain sight, a witness to the unfolding drama.
The distant murmur of voices reached my ears, a growing assembly
of people gathering in the street, their anxiety a palpable undercurrent. I
stepped back through the tower wall.

How did she see me? The question gnawed at my mind, defying
logic. I was certain I had been invisible, a mere wisp in the shadows. I Was
it because I was weak or because of where I was?
***
I walked around the church until I could see the crowd. The
realization that she could see me shouldn't have shocked me; my strength
was a fading ember, a dwindling force that left me vulnerable, exposed, and
questioning my own abilities. It was true, the little strength I had left was
waning. I had never felt so exhausted like I could sleep for a week.
Everything I did drained me further, took more from me, but I couldn’t
stop. I had to help and something inside of me drove me on.
Less than fifty people had gathered in the middle of the street. The
pastor burst from the church doors, his face flushed with panic, his voice
cracking as he shouted: “Indians! Indians are approaching!”
Most in the crowd turned toward him. A few went for their horses
and started down the road. Fear and wonder went through most of the
crowd. Those with children gathered them close. Others mumbled their
disapproval and worry while they waited for action.
A figure, towering over the pastor, stepped forward, his inquiry
slicing through the unsettled air like a blade, demanding attention. His
clothes were neater and obviously more expensive. Where everyone else in
the group seemed to be in desperate need of a laundry - hemlines and cuffs
were covered in road dust, coats were threadbare, and shoes were worn -
this man's attire was pristine, as if freshly tailored, standing in stark contrast
to the worn garments around him.
“Hester Winthrop,” the pastor said.
A ripple of recognition passed through some in the crowd as Hester's
name reached their ears.  “She rang the bell?” The taller man stared at the
church as he spoke.
“Yes, brother. She must have seen them on her way into the village,
she must have…”
“Yes, little brother. She must have passed by a troop of Indians on
their way to attack the village.” He laughed the words and turned to two

men behind him. “Gather the muskets.” The two men turned and headed
toward the largest building of the four, others in the small crowd followed.
“Brother, we can’t fight them. There could be fifty or more.” The
pastor wrung his hands. The worry dripped from every syllable.
“Little brother,” the tall man said, his voice dripping with sarcasm
as he laid a heavy hand on his older brother’s shoulder. “God will protect
his children from the heathens, isn’t that what the Bible says?” The pastor's
face tightened, but he said nothing, his faith unwavering.
He looked at the rest of the people, the women and children who
stared at him waiting for direction. “Come with me, come into the church.
We will be safe there.”
***
Time stood still, stretching seconds into a seemingly endless stretch
of minutes. The air grew charged with an anticipation that hung in the
atmosphere, like an impending storm ready to break free.
Gradually, a sense of unity coalesced from the uncertainty. Between
thirty and forty men, faces etched with determination, formed a human wall
along the street outside the tiny church. Muskets rested in their hands, a
testament to their readiness, while the glint of swords and hunting knives
hinted at a defiance unwilling to yield. Their collective presence painted a
portrait of resolve, a defiance against the encroaching shadows.
Atop the bell tower, I gained a vantage over the forest and beyond.
In truth it was unnecessary. I could feel the anxious excitement of the
raiding party as they approached. Their energy, foreign and unsettling,
tickled across my skin, a sensation far removed from the familiar blue wisps
of sexual energy I knew. It was a new, raw force, and it resonated within
me.
As they reached the village, they spread out across the edge of the
forest. The men on the street heard them moving in but waited for the tall
man to give them orders.
“Did you hear that, Mr. Tucker?” A man with a long mustache and a
musket taller than he was looked up at the crisply-dressed man beside him.
Tucker stared off into the forest between the buildings. “Show
yourselves,” he aimed his musket into the shadows. “You have come to find

your end, you deviled heathens.”
Eyes darted between Tucker and fellow villagers, uncertainty and
fear mirrored in their glances. Of the group, he was obviously the bravest,
the man all of them believed in and his strength held the rest steady.
And then it started.
With a deadly whistle, a hatchet flew from the shadowed forest,
striking Tucker's loyal friend, a man who had grown from a timid recruit to
a courageous fighter. The impact was more than physical; it was a blow to
Tucker's heart, a loss that would shape him into a more hardened and
resolute leader.
The sickening crack of bone resonated through the air, akin to the
snap of a dry branch underfoot. His musket fired and he fell to the dirt with
little more than a grunt.
A deafening cacophony of gunfire erupted, a relentless volley of
fifteen or twenty shots, each one a manifestation of fear, determination, and
a desperate will to survive.
Shouts and cries rang out as Indians sprang from the woods. Their
war cries echoed between the buildings.
The men in front of the church reloaded as fast as they could.
Tucker pulled a small, silver pistol from his belt, fired, and a man fell, face
first, into the dirt.
More shots rang out and more Indians fell and writhed in the street.
A man to Tucker’s left let out a gut-wrenching scream as the long,
thin shaft of an arrow suddenly protruded from his belly, a cruel and
unexpected invader.
“Keep firing!” Tucker yanked the arrow back through the man and
threw it on the ground. “Kill them all!”
I couldn’t bear to watch any longer, the street a nightmarish tableau
of falling bodies, screams of agony, and the relentless clash of battle. The
men in the city fired and reloaded. Indians rushed forward screaming while
other’s fired bows or threw axes. There were far more Indians than
townsmen, but the muskets seemed to hold the Indians back. I couldn’t tell
if they were afraid or if there was some plan, something they were waiting
for. But Tucker didn’t care. He rallied his men, shouting that they fire or

reload or pointing out into the forest wherever he was sure he had seen one
of the painted faces.
Desperation clawed at me. How could I intervene? How could I halt
this tide of violence? Closing my eyes, I focused on the energy around me. I
couldn’t see it, but I could feel it. It was dirty and dark - the strange mix of
bravery and fear of anger and regret. I drew it in, energy coursing through
me.
My essence deepened, shadowed, as if a dark shroud had engulfed
my very soul, casting a chilling pallor over my existence. Anger welled
inside of me. Inhaling deeply, arms outstretched, I manifested atop the bell
tower.
Look at me. I sent the thought through the forest like a wave.
The dark energy of the battle attached to me like tendrils of black
smoke.
The Indians stared up at the bell tower and found my eyes glowing
blue, my robe awash with golden symbols that swirled across the fabric,
and my most disapproving look. I stood as an angry goddess, a figure of
judgment they had never seen before, my wrath spreading through their
ranks like wildfire, igniting terror. Amidst gunfire and screams, they
bellowed, fingers tremblingly aimed at me. Wrath incarnate, I ignited dread,
their panicked flight echoing.
***
When I was sure the majority of the Indians had retreated and the
battle was all but finished, I disappeared. Of the villagers that had fought,
more than half were standing, but the street was littered with dead. Bodies
lay face down in dark brown patches - a mix of dirt and blood, some at odd
angles, rag dolls devoid of life and spirit. A few were wounded. The air was
filled with groans and cries. Where there wasn’t death there was pain and
where there was neither, there was regret and pity.
I ventured into the mind of a dying man, navigating the labyrinth of
his pain and fear, feeling the echoes of his lost dreams and unspoken love
for his family. Each memory was a poignant reminder of a life once vibrant,
now slipping away. I reached into his memories and showed him the joy of

his life, his children, his marriage - there was little else I could do Too weak
to heal, I found myself ill-prepared for this heart of darkness I now
confronted, a stark contrast to my past and rebirth.
I had never been surrounded by death before. I had seen movies, the
gentle Hollywood depictions, but real battle was something different,
something vicious and dark. No victors, only the survivors - stark reality of
a world drenched in the aftermath.
Craving solace, I slipped into the forest's embrace, grappling with
this newfound wellspring of power. Past the lines of fallen Native
Americans, I turned north away from the village and toward the sea. The
trees were almost empty of leaves. The air was cool. Autumn was close.
Every breeze rolled the forest floor, dry leaves tumbled and scraped. I took
a deep breath and watched the forest breath around me. A squirrel clung
upside down to a tree trunk, a raven called out for its mate from a high
branch, and a fallen tree lay like the ancient bones of a great dragon, all part
of the forest's living tapestry.
I caught the scent of the ocean, the salt in the air. It was a smell that
took me back to my childhood, to summers spent on the beach, to bright
sunlight and bathing suits. I stepped onto the shore and dug my toes into the
cool sand. The water gently lapped at my ankles.
As far as the eye could see, the beach was empty. The horizon was
clear of ships. The world around me seemed, for a moment, untouched and
almost new. I drew a deep, shuddering breath, fighting back the sadness that
welled in my eyes and lodged a lump in my throat, a physical manifestation
of my despair.
I didn’t know where I was. I didn’t know how to get back. Muma
had said that time was a hard thing to manipulate even for a god, but Toniat
had made it seem easy. I thought back to the moment I was flying towards
her ready to strike - the look in her eye, that wicked smile, and then in the
space of a heartbeat, she cast me back, flung me through time like a stone.
In my own time, I had been a stranger to my true self. But here, in
this unfamiliar place, my confusion deepened. I sat down on a large gray
boulder, light blue tears rolling down my cheeks, a manifestation of my
despair. I needed help. Without it, I was fairly sure I would wither and
what? Die?

Muma. Muma where are you? I need you, Muma. Can you hear me?
Doubt gnawed at me. Was he even part of this reality? Though my
surroundings resembled the birth of America and the New World, there was
no guarantee that I was in the same reality, or even the same world.
My evening with Muma had taught me that much - that there was
more than one reality, more than the plane of existence that humans
inhabited. No, there were far more from the planes that my kind came from
to others that my kind could no longer reach.
I watched the waves roll onto the shore, their rhythmic dance
mesmerizing. Staring out across the water, I lost myself in the vast expanse,
feeling both connected and adrift. I was getting used to feeling alone,
getting used to having nothing, but I was getting dangerously weak and I
knew it. I had seen Enele weakness when I rescued her and, though I may
not have been as weak, I was heading in the same direction. Without
worship, I would disappear and Toniat would finally have her victory - the
end of her sister and her power - forever.
I need a supplicant, someone to believe in me. I sighed, memories of
Julia flooding my mind. She had given herself to me wholly, completely,
and without question. Her love, her worship, had been a balm to my soul.
Just the thought of her love warmed my insides. It was such a wonderful
thing, the way it felt to be worshiped.
Something called to me. I stood up and looked around. It felt like
something tugging at my soul like there was an invisible bit of string
wrapped around my thoughts and somewhere someone was gently pulling. I
closed my eyes and tried to focus on the sensation, it was new and strange, I
had never felt anything quite like it.
And then I understood. Someone was calling for me. Where is it
coming from? How can I follow it? I turned back toward the south, toward
the village. It was a woman, I could hear her thoughts and I recognized it.
Hester.
***
Smoke rose from a pile of hewn timbers and roughly hewn planks.
There was a body on the ground beside the pile of smolder and smoke. Part
of his head was missing, the rest was charred. The scene looked violent.
Whoever had come through had acted like a storm, a hurricane of death and

destruction. Hester was a few meters from the house and the dead man. She
knelt along the edge of the forest. Her eyes were closed and she was
mumbling words that I both recognized and understood, Latin, an ancient
dialect. I moved closer and saw on the ground in front of her a vine that she
had woven into an ornate pattern - a spiral intersected with a four pointed
star. She had placed a blossom of a red flower on each point of the star.
Magic. The realization struck me like a bolt of lightning. She's
casting a spell. Could that be what brought me here? The connection
between us was undeniable, a mystical force pulling me towards her.
“Goddess of earth, hear my plea,” Hester said.
I stared down at the symbol she had constructed. It was indeed some
sort of magic, there was some strange pull that I had never felt before.
Carefully, I stepped into the center of the red blossoms as if I knew that I
was supposed to.
Hester opened her eyes. “Goddess!” She fell backward until she was
sitting in the grass.
I smiled but did not move. The last thing I wanted to do was scare
her. She didn’t look fragile, not as fragile as I may have been surrounded by
the death and destruction around us, but she definitely hadn’t expected my
arrival.
She stared into my eyes while she collected herself. “Everything
inside of me said that you would come, that you would hear my words and
attend my plea,” she slowly got back to her knees.
I looked around at the smoke and ruin. “He was your husband?”
She stared across the trodden grass. “Yes.”
There was no pain in her voice, no sign of loss or regret. She stared
up at me and started to speak, but the sound of horses cut her off.
From the west came a small wagon pulled by two horses. Two men
sat on the driver’s bench, one was Tucker, the man who had led the band of
villagers and the other was a wiry fellow in his early twenties with multiple
scars across both cheeks. The young man's posture, leaning away from the
rotund, impeccably dressed Tucker, hinted at a painful history. His old
wounds seemed to bear Tucker's signature, a silent testament to their
relationship. When I saw him I immediately thought: henchman.

As soon as Hester saw them, she got to her feet, ignored me, and
started toward them leaving me and the spiral behind.
The wagon came to halt and Tucker stared out across the
devastation.
Hester stopped within earshot but didn’t say a word.
Eventually, Tucker let his eyes fall on the young lady. His face was
emotionless. He took a breath deep enough that his chest expanded with the
effort. “Mrs. Winthrop, it appears God has made you a widow,” Tucker
said, his words short and harsh, like little slaps across Hester's face. His
tone revealed a man accustomed to control, to having everything precisely
as he wanted.
But Hester didn’t flinch. She stood still and stared at the ground.
My curiosity piqued by the woman who had seen me when I thought
she couldn't, and who had summoned me with magic, I tuned into her
thoughts, eager to unravel the mystery of her abilities.
Jacob was a fool for listening to you, a fool for falling into your
trap, but I am not like him. I am no fool.
“What do you have to say for yourself?” Tucker stared down at the
woman from his perch on the wagon.
“Nothing,” the word was quiet, matter of fact, and alone.
“Nothing?” Tucker glanced at the man beside him. “God has seen fit
to punish you, Hester Winthrop and in doing so, he has punished me. You
and your husband owed me a great deal of silver and,” he waved his hand,
“and I see no crop of wheat in the spring, no means for repayment.”
Hester looked up at Tucker, her eyes betraying nothing, but inside
her anger was boiling, murderous thoughts flashing like lightning. I could
hear them, feel the intensity of her rage. He owed you silver. I owe you
nothing.
“Did you hear me, woman?”
Now my own anger was starting to stir. There was no excuse for
such behavior, I didn’t care what year it was - Tucker was proving to be a
bully, but I stayed in the center of the circle of vines and flowers. I wanted
to see what Hester would do. She didn’t look weak, she didn’t look afraid.

Instead, she looked and thought like I felt, like she wanted to end him
where he sat.
“Yes, Mr. Tucker, I understand that Jacob owed you silver. We were
grateful for your kindness when we sought land to build and farm. But to
say that I am responsible for his debts...” Hester's voice trailed off, her eyes
challenging.
“That you are responsible for the debt of your house, lady?”
Hester stared up at the man. Her hands clenched into fists and her
breathing became steady.
“Surely, Mr. Tucker, you can reclaim the land and…”
“I have no interest in your land, Mrs. Winthrop,” Tucker interrupted.
“Your husband signed the deed. The land belongs to you as does the debt.
But,” he waved his hands in the air as if he were erasing their conversation
from the breeze, everything that had been said up until then, “these are
words we will have another time. For now, I am sorry for the loss God has
seen fit to visit upon you. Gideon,” Tucker pointed to the body on the
ground a few meters away, “give Hester a shovel so she that she may bury
her husband and entreat God for mercy.”
The thin man, his face impassive, reached into the back of the
wagon and tossed a shovel at Hester's feet, the metal clanging against the
ground. The metal clanked along the ground and skidded to stop in front of
Hester with a little cloud of dust.
She hadn’t taken her eyes off Tucker. I will see you hang...
Tucker held her glare for a moment before he nodded to the driver
who snapped the reins along the horse’s backs and started them moving.
***
Hester watched the wagon disappear into the forest before she
turned back to me. The anger still lingered, mingled with a burning hatred
for the men in the wagon, yet beneath it all lay a palpable sense of pain and
worry. She walked to the edge of the little symbol I stood inside of and
collapsed to her knees.
“Where did you learn this magic?” I asked, my eyes drawn to the
red flowers and intricate pattern of vines, feeling their pull."
“My mother,” Hester sighed. “She was a secret practitioner.”

“What is it?” I was fascinated by the idea of it, that a simple
arrangement of vines and flowers along with a string of words, had such an
effect on me. All my life I had denied the possibility of the supernatural -
the idea of gods and magic. That there was a power that could summon a
goddess was exciting.
“There are those that call it witchcraft.” Hester stared at the symbols
and edicts that flowed across my robe. “My mother said it was the power of
the earth, that we asked the goddess to bless us and she did.”
I was the goddess of the earth. I was the goddess of love and
happiness. I was the goddess she called upon, the one summoned to her
side.
Everything inside of me wanted to touch her, wanted to take her and
suckle the energy she could provide. My soul felt hungry but I denied the
urge. I was a goddess, a goddess unlike Toniat who kept a temple full of
slaves to give her power. I didn’t need a group of humans who had their
brains scrambled. I would be worshiped by choice or trade. My adherents
would love me. I would not force or betray a human to gain what I needed.
I would not.
“Goddess,” Hester's voice trembled, her smile weak, “I would give
you anything, anything you ask. I am alone, my husband dead, my home
burned. I have nothing left. I cannot even afford passage back to England. I
implore you. Give me any task, ask anything you will of me, I am yours.”
The words bubbled out of her - a mix of fear and respect, of awe and
wonder - but there was something else, she was doing her best to hide it, but
it was there.
I knelt down in the circle, playing along with her game, and took her
face in my hand. Her tears were flowing, but I sensed they were for show. I
could feel it, Hester was stronger than the side she was showing me. No,
she wanted something, I knew it, but I needed her as much as she needed
me. “Ask of me what you will.”
Her eyes widened momentarily at the sensation of my touch, a brief
exchange of energy that I felt too, a connection that went beyond the
physical. But then her spirit emerged from hiding, the darkness of her
hatred shadowed everything and her eyes narrowed. “I want my revenge,”
she hissed, her voice dripping with venom.

Revenge—a feeling I knew all too well.
Revenge. I knew the feeling well. As soon as Hester said the word,
my own lust for it returned. I would have traded anything, including my
very existence to see Toniat destroyed. What Hester felt, I knew well. But
